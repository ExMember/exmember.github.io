Ry,
my [TK year] Thor Rize,
is built on
a 118-in wheelbase, [TK check height]
high-roof [TK year] Ram Promaster.

The Promaster is
a phenomenal choice
for a campervan.
It is less expensive
to purchase
and less expensive
to maintain
than other
campervan chassis.
The short wheelbase
makes it easier
to maneuver
in the urban areas.
And the low floor
makes it easy
to step into
and makes it
so that
there is
more than enough room
to stand up inside,
even thought
it's not exceptionally tall.

That low floor
is possible because
the Ram Promaster is
front-wheel drive only.
Without a driveshaft
to transfer
engine power
to the rear wheel,
the cabin floor
can be lower
to the ground.

This is
a good trade-off
for almost
all uses.
But in my
van-life dream, [TK link]
my van was
a mobile ski lodge.
Getting to
ski resorts
requires driving on
mountain roads
in the winter
ideally with snow
and lots of it.
Four-wheel drive
would make
me feel
a lot more
safe and secure
on those drives.

## Chains

In addition,
when there
is snow
on California mountain roads
chains are
often required.
The most common
[chain-requirement](https://dot.ca.gov/travel/winter-driving-tips/chain-controls)
is R2,
meaning chains
are required
except for all vehicles
that do not
have four-wheel drive.
If you
have ever
pulled over to
the side
of the road
in the snow
to put on
or take off
tire chains
you know
how unpleasant
of an experiences
it is.
Driving
with chains on
is not fun either.
Having four-wheel drive
means skipping
that process
almost every time.

## Off road

Finally,
the best places
to camp inexpensively
near a ski resort
are often
on Forest Service
or BLM land.
But the Forest Service
and BLM
don't pave
their roads.
In winter
the roads
can be
snowy, muddy, and uneven.

Four-wheel drive
greatly reduces
the possibility
of getting stuck.
And getting stuck
is something
I don't want
to risk
when travelling
in the wilderness,
especially when
camping alone. [TK link to someone to travel with]

My campervan needs
four-wheel drive.
